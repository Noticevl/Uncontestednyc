FAQ

What is an uncontested divorce in New York?

An uncontested divorce is one where both spouses agree to end the marriage and agree on all required issues—property division, debt allocation, child custody/parenting time, child support, and (if applicable) spousal support. Because there are no disputes for a judge to decide, the case proceeds on papers instead of courtroom hearings. The court must still review and approve your agreement and the proposed judgment.

Do I need to appear in court?

In a typical NYC uncontested divorce, there are no in‑person court appearances. All documents are filed through NYSCEF (where available) or delivered through the clerk, and the judge reviews your papers in chambers. If something is missing or unclear, we address it by supplemental filings—not a hearing.

How long does an uncontested divorce take in NYC?

Timeframes vary by county and judicial part. Once both spouses sign the documents, many matters finalize in 8–16 weeks. Delays usually come from missing forms, service issues, or heavy court calendars. I set realistic expectations and keep you informed as the clerk’s office and judge move the file.

What does the flat fee cover?

The $5,000 flat fee covers attorney time to evaluate eligibility; draft and review the settlement documents and uncontested divorce packet; coordinate service; file through NYSCEF or EDDS when applicable; cure defects the clerk flags; and monitor the case through entry of Judgment of Divorce. The plan also includes the standard New York filing fees and routine personal service within NYC. Edge cases—translation, out‑of‑state service, publication, extensive revisions—are priced as add‑ons only if needed.

Are court costs really included?

Yes—typical NYC filing fees are included in the flat fee. If a case requires unusual costs (e.g., publication, international service, certified translations), we’ll confirm them in writing before any spend. The goal is zero surprises.

What if we don’t have a signed settlement yet?

That’s fine. I can draft a plain‑English settlement agreement or stipulation that memorializes the terms you already agree on. We’ll focus on clarity and enforceability without unnecessary complexity.

What if we start uncontested but a dispute pops up?

If disagreements arise—say about parenting time or finances—we’ll pause and reassess. I can recommend next steps (mediation, limited‑scope negotiation, or, if truly necessary, contested counsel). You only pay the flat fee if the matter remains uncontested.

How is service of process handled?

New York requires personal service of the Summons on the defendant by a non‑party adult; within NYC we coordinate routine personal service. If a spouse is cooperative, we can often use an affidavit of defendant instead of formal service. When service is challenging, we’ll plan alternatives consistent with CPLR and local rules.

Will a judge review our agreement?

Yes. The court reviews your proposed Judgment, settlement, and required affidavits to confirm they comply with New York law and protect the best interests of any children. If provisions are unclear or incomplete, the clerk or court may request corrections. I build to court‑ready from the start to avoid that.

Can we keep our parenting schedule flexible?

Absolutely. Courts accept customized schedules if they are specific enough to be enforceable and protect the children’s best interests. Many parents use a baseline schedule with agreed flexibility language. I’ll propose language that avoids ambiguity while giving you room to adapt.

How does child support work?

The Child Support Standards Act (CSSA) sets a formula based on combined parental income and the number of children, with adjustments for health insurance, childcare, and add‑ons. You can deviate from the formula if you both agree and the court finds the result fair. I provide a worksheet so you understand the default and any negotiated alternative.

What about spousal support (maintenance)?

New York has advisory guidelines based on income and certain caps. Many uncontested divorces use a short‑term or waived maintenance depending on circumstances. If you’re considering maintenance, I’ll run the calculator and translate the impact into plain English.

Do we need to split retirement accounts now?

If retirement assets are part of the settlement, we usually reference them in the agreement and then finalize transfers with a QDRO (for certain plans) after Judgment. I’ll flag when a QDRO specialist is prudent to ensure the plan accepts the order.

Can we keep the apartment/house while we sell or refinance?

Yes. Many agreements set an interim plan—who lives there, who pays carrying costs, timelines to list or refinance, and how net proceeds are divided. Specifics prevent later disputes.

Can I restore a former name?

Yes. You can ask the court to restore a prior surname in the Judgment of Divorce. I include the appropriate clause so your identification updates are straightforward.

What are the most common reasons papers get rejected?

Missing signatures, inconsistent dates, unclear parenting provisions, incorrect service windows, and not attaching required forms (especially in child‑related matters). My process includes a defect‑check pass before filing.

Will the divorce affect immigration status?

Divorce can interact with certain immigration benefits. I don’t provide immigration advice, but I’ll flag issues and recommend consulting an immigration attorney when needed.

Can we file if a spouse lives outside New York?

Yes, depending on New York’s residency requirements. Many cases proceed when one spouse meets the residency rule. We’ll confirm eligibility in the consult.

Do we need notarization?

Yes. Many documents must be notarized. I’m set up for remote online notarization (RON) where permissible, or we can arrange convenient in‑person notarization.

What if we have overseas documents?

If documents originate abroad, the court may require apostilles or certified translations. We’ll identify those early to avoid delay.
